01 The Problem

A Rules Engine That Couldn't Keep Up

Every month, over 500,000 payment transactions moved through the organization's systems and for a long time, there was no reliable way to tell which ones were fraudulent in real time. The existing process was largely manual and rule-based: flag anything over a certain dollar amount, block specific merchant categories, review anything that tripped a predefined threshold.

At lower volumes that worked passably. At 500K+ transactions a month, the cracks were impossible to ignore. The rules engine generated a high volume of alerts, but a significant portion were false positives legitimate transactions flagged because they happened to meet a static criterion that didn't account for actual behavioral context.

The core tension: The fraud team was spending the majority of its day clearing false alarms legitimate card use on road trips, routine high-value purchases, normal merchant patterns instead of investigating real threats. Actual fraud was slower to surface as a result.

On the billing side, incorrectly flagged transactions were occasionally causing billing errors charges held or reversed when they shouldn't have been creating friction with clients and surfacing in financial control reviews. Compliance was a concern too: the organization needed a demonstrably rigorous and auditable monitoring process, and a manually managed rules engine wasn't providing that.

① Building the Data Foundation in SQL

Before any detection logic could work, the data had to be reliable and unified. At the credit union, transaction records lived across a core banking system and a separate card processing vendor that didn't share a common account identifier. I built SQL pipelines in PostgreSQL to reconcile these sources into a single transaction schema, pulling in 18 months of clean history.

Feature engineering was where the real analytical work happened. Fraud isn't usually one unusual transaction it's a pattern. I built SQL aggregation tables computing behavioral baselines per account and per merchant across 7-day, 30-day, and 90-day rolling windows: spend deviation from historical mean, transaction velocity, merchant category patterns, time-of-day distributions, and geographic signals. These features became the inputs to the models and refreshed on a scheduled pipeline cadence.

The SQL layer also handled billing reconciliation cross-checking flagged and reversed transactions against billing records to surface any cases where a flag had incorrectly affected a client. This gave the finance team a reliable automated audit trail in place of manual month-end reconciliation.

② Anomaly Detection Models in Python

Isolation Forest was the primary model. It works by randomly partitioning the feature space and identifying transactions that are isolated quickly outliers that don't cluster naturally with normal behavior. It required no labeled fraud examples to train on; it learns what normal looks like and flags deviations. It was well-suited for transactions where fraud labels were sparse.

Z-score analysis ran as a complementary univariate check, flagging transactions where a single dimension most often dollar amount deviated significantly from an account's own historical mean. This caught cases where everything else looked normal but the amount was sharply out of character. Together, the two methods covered more ground than either alone.

Design decision: Both models produced a continuous risk score (0–100) rather than a binary flag. A score gives analysts something to triage with: a 92 warrants immediate action, a 61 goes in the review queue. This was a deliberate choice to reduce noise and give the fraud team control over their own threshold management.

③ Tableau Dashboards for Two Different Audiences

The fraud operations team got a daily triage workbench: high-risk transactions ranked by score, with the key behavioral features driving each alert visible in one view. An analyst could understand why a transaction was flagged and make a decision without opening three separate systems. The executive and compliance team got a separate view fraud rate trends, false positive rate on a 90-day rolling basis, total volume monitored, and a billing accuracy tracker for financial control reviews.
